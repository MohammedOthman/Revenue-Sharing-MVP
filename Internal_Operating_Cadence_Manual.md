
# Partner Revenue OS — Internal Operating Cadence Manual
## End-to-End Company Workflow, Meeting Rhythm, Handoffs, Decision System, and Alignment Model

---

## 0. Purpose of This MD

This document defines the internal operating cadence for the company building **Partner Revenue OS**.

The goal is to create a simple, practical, end-to-end way for every function in the company to work together:

- Founder / CEO
- Product
- Engineering
- Design
- QA / Security
- GTM / Sales
- Marketing
- Solution Consulting
- Customer Success
- Implementation
- RevOps
- Finance
- Legal / Admin / HR
- Advisors / Board

The operating cadence exists to make sure the company does not become chaotic while building a product that itself solves revenue chaos for customers.

The product is a **Partner Revenue OS**. It helps customers control partner revenue through:

- Partner Revenue Claim Ledger
- Partner registry
- Deal / claim submission
- Attribution workflow
- Protection windows
- Agreement metadata
- Payout eligibility preview
- Evidence archive
- Dispute management
- Audit log
- Partner dashboard
- Executive reporting
- Future CRM / ERP / billing integrations

Product phases map to the canonical model in `README.md`: Phase 1 — PRM / Capture (MVP + V1) ·
Phase 2 — Settle (V2) · Phase 3 — Orchestrate (V3). The 18-month plan in this manual executes
inside **Phase 1**. In Phase 1 the product calculates and reports payout eligibility only — it
does not execute payments, move funds, or perform settlement; those are gated Phase 2–3
capabilities (see the Integration Layer and API Data Flows Manual, §20).

This means the company must operate with strong internal discipline. The same logic we sell to customers — clarity, accountability, evidence, cadence, and control — must exist inside our own company.

# 1. The Operating Philosophy

## 1.1 The company must run on cadence, not memory

In early-stage companies, many things live inside founders’ heads.

That works for a few weeks.

It fails when the company has:

- product roadmap decisions,
- engineering delivery,
- customer pilots,
- GTM pipeline,
- implementation tasks,
- customer support,
- cash burn,
- hiring,
- legal contracts,
- investor updates,
- and customer feedback happening at the same time.

The internal cadence makes sure the company knows:

```text
What are we trying to achieve?
Who owns it?
What changed?
What is blocked?
What decision is needed?
What will happen next?
```

## Rationale

A startup dies from confusion as often as it dies from lack of effort.

The operating cadence prevents silent drift.

---

## 1.2 The company should run on evidence, not opinions

Every function should bring evidence.

Examples:

| Function | Evidence to bring |
|---|---|
| Product | User feedback, usage data, roadmap tradeoffs |
| Engineering | Delivery status, bugs, technical risk |
| Sales | Pipeline, next steps, objections, deal stage |
| Marketing | Content performance, message resonance, inbound signals |
| CS | Activation milestones, customer blockers, value proof |
| Finance | Burn, runway, cash receipts, budget variance |
| RevOps | Funnel conversion, CRM hygiene, forecast accuracy |
| Legal/Admin | Contract status, procurement blockers, compliance needs |

## Rationale

Opinions create arguments.

Evidence creates decisions.

---

## 1.3 The company should run on clear ownership

Every important task must have one accountable owner.

Many people can help.

Only one person owns the outcome.

Use this simple ownership language:

| Role type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Accountable | The person ultimately responsible for the result |
| Responsible | The person doing the work |
| Consulted | People who provide input before a decision |
| Informed | People who need to know after the decision |

## Rationale

When everyone owns something, nobody owns it.

Clear ownership prevents delays, duplicated work, and hidden assumptions.

---

## 1.4 The company should run on short loops

The company should not wait a month to find out that:

- a customer is blocked,
- a sprint is delayed,
- a sales opportunity is stuck,
- cash burn is higher than planned,
- implementation is failing,
- or a feature is not being used.

The cadence should create short feedback loops:

```text
Daily: unblock work
Weekly: align functions
Monthly: inspect metrics and cash
Quarterly: reset strategy and goals
```

## Rationale

Short loops allow the team to correct direction before mistakes become expensive.

# 2. The Operating System Structure

The company should run through five connected rhythms:

```text
1. Daily execution rhythm
2. Weekly functional rhythm
3. Weekly cross-functional leadership rhythm
4. Monthly business review rhythm
5. Quarterly strategy and planning rhythm
```

Each rhythm has a different purpose.

| Rhythm | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Daily | Remove blockers and keep work moving |
| Weekly functional | Manage team-level execution |
| Weekly cross-functional | Align product, GTM, CS, finance, and leadership |
| Monthly | Review business performance and make resource decisions |
| Quarterly | Set priorities, reset OKRs, allocate capital, and adjust strategy |

# 3. Company North Star and Operating Goals

## 3.1 North Star

The company’s internal North Star should be:

```text
Activated Partner Revenue OS Customers
```

An activated customer is not just a signed customer.

An activated customer is a customer that has:

- uploaded partner data,
- configured claim workflow,
- submitted real partner claims,
- reviewed claims,
- made attribution decisions,
- applied protection windows,
- generated payout eligibility previews,
- used dashboards,
- and started an operating cadence around partner revenue.

## Rationale

ARR is important, but ARR without activation creates churn risk.

Activated customers prove that the product is actually becoming part of the customer’s operating model.

---

## 3.2 Company-Level Goals

The company should track five company goals at all times:

| Goal | Plain-English meaning |
|---|---|
| Build the right product | Product solves the real workflow pain |
| Sell to the right customers | GTM targets customers with urgent pain |
| Activate customers fast | Customers reach first value quickly |
| Keep burn controlled | Cash is spent on proof, not noise |
| Create reference proof | Customers become proof for the next sale |

## Rationale

These five goals connect product, GTM, CS, and finance into one system.

# 5. Daily Operating Cadence

## 5.1 Daily Founder / Leadership Check

### Purpose

Identify the top company blockers for the day.

### Frequency

Daily, 10–15 minutes.

### Participants

- CEO / Founder
- CTO / Product lead
- GTM lead if active
- CS/Implementation lead if customers are live

### Questions

1. What is the most important company outcome this week?
2. What is blocked today?
3. Which customer or deal needs founder attention?
4. Which product issue threatens delivery or activation?
5. Which cash, legal, or hiring issue needs a decision?
6. What must be done before end of day?

### Output

A short list of daily unblock actions.

### Rationale

Founders should not spend the day reacting randomly. This check creates focus.

---

## 5.2 Daily Engineering Standup

### Purpose

Keep product delivery moving.

### Frequency

Daily during active build periods.

### Participants

- CTO / Engineering lead
- Engineers
- Product manager or founder product owner
- QA if active

### Questions

1. What did we complete yesterday?
2. What are we building today?
3. What is blocked?
4. Is anything threatening the sprint goal?
5. Are any bugs blocking customer usage?
6. Are any security or data issues urgent?

### Output

- Updated sprint board
- Blocker list
- Engineering decision needs

### Rationale

The product has multiple workflow-heavy components. Daily engineering clarity prevents delays from hiding.

---

## 5.3 Daily Customer Blocker Review

### Purpose

Make sure active pilots and customers do not get stuck.

### Frequency

Daily if customers are in implementation; otherwise 2–3 times weekly.

### Participants

- Customer Success / Implementation owner
- Product
- Engineering if needed
- Sales if the customer is still in pilot/commercial stage

### Questions

1. Which customer is blocked?
2. What data is missing?
3. What workflow is not configured?
4. Which user is not trained?
5. Which bug blocks activation?
6. What decision do we need from the customer?
7. Who will contact the customer today?

### Output

- Customer blocker tracker
- Owner for each blocker
- Customer next-step message

### Rationale

Early customers are fragile. If they stall, the company loses value proof and references.

---

# 6. Weekly Functional Cadence

## 6.1 Weekly Product and Engineering Planning

### Purpose

Plan what will be built, why it matters, and what must ship.

### Frequency

Weekly, 45–60 minutes.

### Participants

- Product owner
- CTO
- Engineering
- Design
- QA
- CS representative
- Sales representative when customer feedback affects roadmap

### Agenda

1. Review current sprint progress.
2. Review blockers.
3. Review customer-impacting bugs.
4. Review upcoming release items.
5. Review product feedback from GTM and CS.
6. Decide what stays in scope and what moves out.
7. Confirm release date and owner.

### Required Inputs

- Sprint board
- Bug list
- Customer feedback log
- Roadmap priorities
- Customer implementation blockers

### Outputs

- Confirmed weekly build priorities
- Updated sprint scope
- Product decisions
- Risks and tradeoffs

### Rationale

The product must stay focused on the core wedge: Partner Revenue Claim Ledger, attribution workflow, protection windows, agreement logic, payout eligibility, and auditability.

Without weekly product discipline, the roadmap will expand too fast.

---

## 6.2 Weekly GTM Pipeline Review

### Purpose

Understand whether the company is creating, advancing, and closing the right opportunities.

### Frequency

Weekly, 45–60 minutes.

### Participants

- Founder / CEO
- Sales / GTM owner
- Marketing

- Solution consulting if active
- Finance if pricing or contracts are involved

### Agenda

1. New target accounts added.
2. Outreach performance.
3. New qualified conversations.
4. Opportunities by stage.
5. Deals stuck and why.
6. Next steps for every active opportunity.
7. Founder help needed.
8. Pricing or legal blockers.
9. Forecast changes.

### Required Inputs

- Account list
- Outreach metrics
- Opportunity notes

### Outputs

- Updated forecast

Pipeline without next steps is fake pipeline.

This review makes sure every opportunity is either moving, being revived, or being disqualified.

## 10.2 Quarterly OKR Planning

### Purpose

Set focused objectives for the next quarter.

### Simple OKR Rule

Use 3–5 company objectives.

Each objective should have 2–4 measurable key results.

### Example Company OKRs

## Objective 1: Prove the MVP solves real partner revenue workflow pain

Key results:

- Process 100 real Partner Revenue Claims across pilot customers.
- Achieve 70%+ activation score for 3 customers.
- Ship attribution workflow, protection window, and payout preview.

## Objective 2: Prove early commercial demand

Key results:

- Close 5 paid pilots or customers.
- Generate SAR X in booked ARR.
- Maintain average sales cycle below X days.
- Build pipeline coverage of 3x next quarter bookings target.

## Objective 3: Build repeatable implementation

Key results:

- Reduce time to first claim to under 14 days.
- Complete implementation playbook.
- Achieve implementation margin of X%.

## Objective 4: Maintain burn discipline

Key results:

- Keep monthly net burn below SAR X.
- Maintain runway above X months.
- Collect at least X% of signed contract cash within 30 days.

### Rationale

OKRs align the whole company around a small number of measurable outcomes.

# 12. Cross-Functional Handoffs

## 12.1 Marketing to Sales

### Trigger

A target account engages or a campaign creates a qualified signal.

### Handoff includes

- account name,
- persona,
- content engaged,
- pain signal,
- recommended outreach angle,
- ICP score.

### Rationale

Sales should not start cold when marketing already has context.

---

## 12.2 Sales to Solution Consulting

### Trigger

Opportunity is qualified and needs diagnostic/demo support.

### Handoff includes

- pain summary,
- stakeholders,
- current process,
- current tools,
- suspected workflow gaps,
- demo objective,
- risks.

### Rationale

Solution consulting should not run a generic demo. It must understand the customer’s workflow.

---

## 12.3 Sales to Customer Success

### Trigger

Pilot or contract is signed.

### Handoff includes

- customer objective,
- scope,
- success criteria,
- stakeholders,
- promises made,
- product modules included,
- data needed,
- timeline,
- risks,
- commercial terms.

### Rationale

CS should not discover the deal from the customer. A clean handoff protects trust.

## 12.4 Customer Success to Product

### Handoff includes

- customer name,
- workflow affected,
- user role affected,
- severity,
- revenue impact,
- frequency,
- workaround,
- recommended priority.

### Rationale

Product should prioritize repeatable customer blockers, not every custom request.

---

## 12.5 Product to Marketing/Sales

New feature, release, or workflow improvement ships.

- what changed,
- who benefits,
- how to explain it,
- demo path,
- customer use case,
- limitations.

### Rationale

Sales and marketing need clear language to turn product progress into market value.

## 12.6 Finance to Leadership

Monthly review or major cash/spend change.

- burn,
- runway,
- collections,
- budget variance,
- hiring affordability,
- funding risk,
- recommended decision.

### Rationale

Leadership needs finance insight before making hiring and GTM decisions.

# 16. Meeting Rules

## 16.1 Every meeting must have a purpose

No meeting should exist because “we always do it.”

Every meeting needs:

- owner,
- agenda,
- inputs,
- outputs,
- decisions,
- next actions.

## 16.2 Every meeting must produce action

If a meeting produces no decision, no learning, and no owner, it should be removed.

## 16.3 Written updates before meetings

For major meetings, each function should submit a short written update before the call.

This reduces meeting time and increases clarity.

## 16.4 No hidden blockers

If something blocks a customer, release, deal, or cash collection, it must be raised immediately.

## 16.5 Decisions must be written down

Decisions should go into the decision log with rationale.

# 18. The End-to-End Internal Workflow

## Step 1 — Strategy sets focus

The company decides:

- target ICP,
- product wedge,
- quarterly goals,
- capital constraints,
- hiring gates.

## Rationale

Without focus, every function will chase different priorities.

---

## Step 2 — GTM tests the market

Sales and marketing test:

- which accounts respond,
- which pain resonates,
- which stakeholders care,
- which objections repeat.

## Rationale

Market response tells the company whether the strategy is real.

---

## Step 3 — Product listens to GTM and CS

Product reviews:

- discovery notes,
- demo feedback,
- implementation blockers,
- usage behavior,
- customer requests.

## Rationale

Product decisions should come from real customer workflow evidence.

---

## Step 4 — Engineering builds the highest-value workflow

Engineering prioritizes:

- claim ledger,
- attribution,
- protection,
- agreement metadata,
- payout preview,
- audit log,
- activation blockers.

## Rationale

Engineering must build the core operating value before expansion features.

---

## Step 5 — Sales closes only qualified customers

Sales should close customers that:

- match ICP,
- have urgent pain,
- have stakeholder access,
- can pay,
- can implement,
- can become proof.

## Rationale

Bad-fit customers create product distraction and implementation drag.

---

## Step 6 — CS implements with a clear checklist

CS runs:

- kickoff,
- data setup,
- workflow setup,
- role training,
- first workflow launch,
- activation tracking.

## Rationale

Customer activation should be managed like a project, not left to chance.

---

## Step 7 — Finance checks whether the system is economical

Finance tracks:

- burn,
- cash,
- ARR,
- collections,
- CAC,
- implementation margin,
- gross margin,
- runway.

## Rationale

Growth without financial control can destroy the company.

---

## Step 8 — Leadership decides what to scale

Leadership reviews:

- product progress,
- customer activation,
- GTM conversion,
- economics,
- risks.

Then decides:

- hire,
- cut,
- defer,
- build,
- sell,
- expand,
- raise.

## Rationale

The operating cadence exists to turn learning into decisions.

---

# 19. Final Internal Operating Principle

The company should operate exactly like the product philosophy it sells.

The product tells customers:

```text
Do not run partner revenue through scattered memory, spreadsheets, and informal approvals.
Run it through a governed operating system.
```

The company should tell itself:

```text
Do not run the startup through scattered conversations, founder memory, and informal priorities.
Run it through a clear operating cadence.
```

The company’s internal cadence is the management system that turns:

```text
product work
+ GTM learning
+ customer activation
+ financial discipline
+ strategic decisions
```

into one aligned company.
