# Partner Revenue OS  
# Large Enterprise Client Onboarding Manual  
## From Outreach → Closing → Implementation → Customer Success

**Document purpose:**  
This manual is for onboarding every person inside the company — founders, CEO, product, engineering, GTM, RevOps, implementation, customer success, finance, and security/compliance — so everyone understands how one large enterprise client should move through the full Partner Revenue OS journey.

**Product strategy reminder:**  
Partner Revenue OS is **not** a *generic* PRM, partner portal, or dashboard product. It is experienced and sold as an easy, claim-centric PRM; engineered underneath as a **Partner Revenue Control Layer** — never a generic PRM, never a fintech in Phase 1.

> **Phase discipline (governing):** This manual's 25 phases (§6) describe the **customer journey**, not the product roadmap. The entire journey through Phase 24 (Executive Value Review) runs on **Product Phase 1 — Capture** (the PRM: claims, attribution, protection, payout-readiness *preview*, evidence; **no payout execution, settlement automation, escrow, or automated ZATCA clearance**). Customer Phase 25 (Expansion, §35.3 "deeper finance workflow") is where **Product Phase 2 — Settle** capabilities enter, and only after the Phase-1 exit gate. See `ROADMAP_ALIGNMENT_AUDIT.md` §2.

The core object is the **Partner Revenue Claim**.

The MVP wedge is:

> **Partner Revenue Claim Ledger + Attribution Integrity**

The system helps enterprises govern partner-generated revenue through:

- Partner Revenue Claims
- Partner registry
- Claim/deal submission
- Attribution workflow
- Protection windows
- Agreement metadata
- Payout eligibility preview
- Finance evidence
- Disputes
- Audit log
- Partner dashboards
- Executive reporting
- Future CRM / ERP / billing integrations

# 3. The Mental Model Everyone Must Understand

## 3.1 We are not selling a dashboard

A dashboard shows information.

Partner Revenue OS creates operational control.

A dashboard might answer:

> “How much partner revenue do we have?”

Partner Revenue OS answers:

> “Which partner revenue claims are real, evidenced, protected, attributable, finance-ready, disputed, rejected, payable, or risky?”

That is a different product category.

## 3.2 We are not selling a generic PRM

A generic PRM may focus on:

- partner onboarding;
- training;
- enablement content;
- partner portals;
- marketing development funds;
- partner communications;
- certifications;
- partner engagement.

Partner Revenue OS focuses on:

- revenue claims;
- attribution;
- evidence;
- approval;
- disputes;
- finance visibility;
- auditability;
- payout eligibility *(preview/calculation in Phase 1 — execution is a Phase-2 Settle capability)*;
- executive control.

*(The buyer should still experience the entry product as an easy, claim-centric PRM — that is the adoption wedge. What we reject is the generic PRM category, not the PRM-shaped product.)*

## 3.3 The real buyer pain

Large enterprises do not wake up wanting another SaaS login.

They care when partner revenue becomes:

- unclear;
- disputed;
- manually reconciled;
- politically sensitive;
- financially risky;
- hard to report;
- hard to audit;
- hard to trust.

The product exists because partner revenue often grows faster than the internal governance process.

## 3.4 The company promise

The company promise is:

> We help large enterprises make partner-generated revenue visible, governable, attributable, finance-ready, and trusted.

## 3.5 The core operating question

Every team member should ask:

> Does this action improve the customer’s ability to govern Partner Revenue Claims?

If the answer is no, it may be distraction.

# 10. The Partner Revenue Claim Lifecycle

This lifecycle is the center of the entire company.

Every founder, employee, engineer, seller, and customer-facing team member must understand it.

## 10.1 What is a Partner Revenue Claim?

A Partner Revenue Claim is a structured record that says:

> A partner believes they sourced, influenced, introduced, co-sold, or contributed to a revenue opportunity and may deserve recognition, protection, attribution, or payout.

A claim must not be treated as “just a lead.”

A claim is a governance object.

It may affect:

- sales ownership;
- partner trust;
- commission or payout;
- revenue reporting;
- dispute resolution;
- finance approval;
- executive reporting.

## 10.2 Full lifecycle

| Step | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 | Partner is registered |
| 2 | Agreement metadata is attached |
| 3 | Partner submits claim |
| 4 | Evidence is attached |
| 5 | Completeness check happens |
| 6 | Protection window is checked |
| 7 | Duplicate/conflict check happens |
| 8 | Attribution is reviewed |
| 9 | Sales approves, rejects, or requests clarification |
| 10 | Finance sees payout eligibility preview |
| 11 | Dispute is opened if needed |
| 12 | Final decision is logged |
| 13 | Executive reporting updates |
| 14 | Learning loop informs product, CS, and expansion |

## 10.3 Claim statuses

Recommended statuses:

- Draft
- Submitted
- Missing Evidence
- Needs Clarification
- Under Review
- Attribution Review
- Sales Review
- Finance Eligibility Review
- Disputed
- Approved
- Approved — Payout Pending
- Partially Approved
- Rejected
- Closed — No Payout
- Closed — Paid Outside System
- Archived

## 10.4 Why statuses matter

Statuses turn messy human judgment into visible workflow.

Without statuses:

- nobody knows what is stuck;
- finance enters too late;
- partners lose trust;
- disputes disappear into messages;
- reporting becomes unreliable.

## 10.5 Evidence examples

Evidence can include:

- email introduction;
- meeting note;
- CRM record;
- customer confirmation;
- call summary;
- signed partner agreement;
- proposal reference;
- contract reference;
- invoice reference;
- sales manager note;
- partner-submitted document.

## 10.6 Attribution types

Recommended attribution types:

- Sourced
- Influenced
- Co-sold
- Introduced
- Expanded
- Renewal-influenced
- Not attributable
- Requires review

## 10.7 Protection window

A protection window defines the period during which a partner may be protected or eligible for attribution after a qualifying action.

Examples of clock-start events:

- claim submission date;
- customer introduction date;
- first meeting date;
- opportunity creation date;
- accepted claim date.

The company must define this clearly for each customer.

## 10.8 Finance eligibility

Finance eligibility is not the same as payment execution.

Finance eligibility means:

> Based on the claim status, evidence, agreement rules, revenue event, and payout conditions, this claim appears potentially payable or not payable.

This is important because the MVP does not need to become a payment system immediately.

It needs to create finance visibility.

# 11. Phase 1 — Account Selection

## Objective

Decide whether this large enterprise account is worth pursuing.

## Why this phase exists

Large enterprises can consume months of founder time.  
The company must avoid chasing big names that do not have urgent partner revenue governance pain.

## Owner

CEO with GTM and RevOps.

## Required inputs

- Company profile
- Partner ecosystem signs
- Public partner program information
- Industry model
- Revenue model
- Existing relationships
- Possible stakeholder list
- Strategic logo value
- Implementation complexity estimate

## Step 1.1 — Build the account hypothesis

### Micro-steps

1. Identify whether the enterprise sells through partners.
2. Identify which partner types likely exist.
3. Estimate whether partner revenue is material.
4. Check whether partner payouts are likely.
5. Check whether multiple business units exist.
6. Check whether partner claims could conflict with sales.
7. Check whether finance likely reviews partner payouts.
8. Check whether the company likely uses CRM.
9. Check whether current partner process may be manual.
10. Decide if the account is likely to feel pain now or later.

### Output

One-page account hypothesis.

## Step 1.2 — Score the account

Score each dimension from 1 to 5.

| Dimension | Score |
|---|---|
| Partner revenue materiality | 1–5 |
| Claim ambiguity | 1–5 |
| Finance involvement | 1–5 |
| Manual process risk | 1–5 |
| Executive urgency | 1–5 |
| Implementation feasibility | 1–5 |
| Expansion potential | 1–5 |
| Strategic logo value | 1–5 |

### Interpretation

- 32+ = Tier 1
- 24–31 = pursue if warm path exists
- Below 24 = nurture

## Step 1.3 — Decide pursuit level

### Pursuit levels

# 38. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

## Mistake 1 — Selling as a PRM

### Problem

The customer compares you to feature-heavy PRMs.

### Fix

Position as a Partner Revenue Control Layer.

---

## Mistake 2 — Demoing too early

### Problem

The demo becomes generic.

### Fix

Diagnose workflow first, then demo around their claim lifecycle.

---

## Mistake 3 — Ignoring finance

### Problem

The partnership team likes it, but budget stalls.

### Fix

Bring finance into the business case early.

---

## Mistake 4 — Allowing unclear pilot scope

### Problem

The pilot becomes unlimited consulting.

### Fix

Define partner type, business unit, workflow, users, data, reports, integrations, and success metrics.

---

## Mistake 5 — Treating implementation as admin setup

### Problem

The customer does not adopt.

### Fix

Treat implementation as operating model configuration.

---

## Mistake 6 — Overbuilding integrations early

### Problem

Engineering gets trapped before value is proven.

### Fix

Use import/export or light sync for pilot unless integration is essential.

---

## Mistake 7 — Weak handoff

### Problem

Implementation does not know what sales promised.

### Fix

Use mandatory sales-to-implementation handoff pack.

---

## Mistake 8 — Tracking logins instead of value

### Problem

CS cannot prove business impact.

### Fix

Track claims, evidence, disputes, finance usage, cycle time, and executive reporting.

---

## Mistake 9 — No executive value review

### Problem

The product stays at user level and does not expand.

### Fix

Run executive value review within 30–45 days.

---

## Mistake 10 — Saying yes to every custom request

### Problem

Product becomes a custom services business.

### Fix

Classify requests as pilot-critical, expansion, future roadmap, or out-of-scope.
