
# Partner Revenue OS — Monthly CFO Review Manual
## Pre-Seed CFO Checklist, Questions, Analysis Pack, and Decision System

---

## 0. Purpose of This MD

This document is the **Monthly CFO Review Manual** for the Partner Revenue OS business.

It is designed for a pre-seed B2B SaaS company building a **Partner Revenue OS**: a product that helps companies govern partner revenue through Partner Revenue Claims, attribution workflows, protection windows, agreement rules, payout eligibility, evidence, audit trails, and executive reporting.

Product phases referenced in this manual map to the canonical model in `README.md`
(Phase 1 — PRM / Capture = MVP + V1 · Phase 2 — Settle = V2 · Phase 3 — Orchestrate = V3); the
pre-seed period funds **Phase 1 only**. "Payout eligibility preview" in the MVP module list means
calculation and reporting only — the product does not execute payments, move funds, or perform
settlement in Phase 1, which is why this budget carries no payment-rail, payments-licensing, or
money-transmission cost lines by design. For the phased integration roadmap see the Integration
Layer and API Data Flows Manual, §20.

The monthly CFO review is not only an accounting meeting.

It is the monthly control system that answers:

```text
Did this month’s cash burn create enough product proof, customer proof, revenue proof, activation proof, and investor proof?
```

The company is in a pre-seed phase. That means the main financial question is not short-term profitability.

The main question is:

```text
Are we using limited cash to create the right proof for the next funding round?
```

# 1. CFO Review Philosophy for This Business

## 1.1 What the CFO review must control

The CFO review must control seven things:

| Control area | Plain-English meaning |
|---|---|
| Cash survival | Do we have enough cash to keep operating? |
| Burn discipline | Are we spending on proof, or wasting cash? |
| Product proof | Is the product becoming real and usable? |
| Customer proof | Are real customers willing to pay and use it? |
| GTM proof | Are we learning how to repeatedly find and close customers? |
| Activation proof | Are customers reaching real operational value? |
| Investor proof | Are we building evidence for the next raise? |

## Rationale

Pre-seed companies do not have unlimited time or money. Every month must produce evidence that the business deserves more capital.

---

## 1.2 The CFO review should not be only backward-looking

A normal finance review asks:

```text
What did we spend?
What revenue did we make?
What is the cash balance?
```

That is not enough.

This CFO review must also ask:

```text
What did the spend produce?
What changed in our proof?
What should we stop spending on?
What should we protect?
What should we accelerate?
What investor milestone are we closer to?
```

## Rationale

At pre-seed, the most important job of finance is not bookkeeping. It is capital allocation.

---

## 1.3 The pre-seed finance principle

The core principle is:

```text
Burn must buy proof.
```

Burn is healthy when it buys:

- working MVP,
- validated ICP,
- paying customers,
- customer activation,
- implementation repeatability,
- referenceable accounts,
- better unit economics,
- stronger fundraising story.

Burn is dangerous when it buys:

- premature headcount,
- vague marketing,
- custom features,
- low-quality pilots,
- weak-fit customers,
- idle sales capacity,
- tools nobody uses,
- integrations before demand is proven.

# 2. Monthly CFO Review Meeting Design

## 2.1 Meeting purpose

The monthly CFO review exists to make clear decisions about:

- runway,
- burn,
- hiring,
- GTM spend,
- product investment,
- implementation capacity,
- pricing,
- customer quality,
- fundraising readiness.

## 2.2 Meeting frequency

Hold this review monthly.

In the first 6 months, the founder should also review cash weekly.

## 2.3 Meeting duration

Recommended duration:

```text
90 to 120 minutes
```

## 2.4 Participants

| Participant | Why they attend |
|---|---|
| CEO / Founder | Owns capital allocation and strategy |
| Finance owner / CFO advisor | Owns cash, burn, reporting, model |
| CTO / Product lead | Explains product progress and R&D spend |
| GTM / Sales lead | Explains pipeline, bookings, conversion |
| Customer Success / Implementation lead | Explains activation and delivery cost |
| RevOps | Explains funnel quality and CRM/reporting accuracy |
| Marketing | Explains demand, education, content, and CAC inputs |
| Legal/Admin | Explains contract, procurement, compliance, payroll, VAT issues |
| Advisor / board observer | Optional, for discipline and accountability |

## Rationale

The CFO review must include every function because cash burn is created by every function.

# 5. Section A — Cash, Runway, and Funding Control

## A1. What is our current cash balance?

### Analysis needed

Track:

- opening cash,
- cash collected,
- cash spent,
- ending cash,
- restricted cash if any,
- VAT cash held but not owned,
- committed but unpaid expenses.

### Rationale

Cash balance is the company’s oxygen. If cash is misunderstood, every decision becomes dangerous.

### CFO decision

Decide whether the company can maintain current spend, must slow spending, or needs to raise earlier.

---

## A2. What is our gross burn this month?

### Analysis needed

Gross burn should include:

- payroll,
- contractors,
- cloud,
- AI/API,
- GTM,
- legal,
- accounting,
- office/admin,
- tools,
- implementation costs,
- customer success,
- taxes/VAT remittance where applicable.

### Formula

```text
Gross Burn = Total cash operating outflow before customer cash receipts
```

### Rationale

Gross burn shows the true monthly cost base before revenue helps.

### CFO decision

Identify whether the operating cost base is growing before proof exists.

---

## A3. What is our net burn this month?

### Analysis needed

Net burn should subtract actual customer cash receipts, not signed contracts.

### Formula

```text
Net Burn = Gross Burn - Customer Cash Receipts
```

### Rationale

Net burn shows the actual cash loss for the month.

### CFO decision

If net burn is rising faster than customer cash receipts, review hiring and GTM spend.

---

## A4. How many months of runway do we have?

### Analysis needed

Calculate runway using current burn and forward 3-month expected burn.

### Formula

```text
Runway = Ending Cash / Average Forward 3-Month Net Burn
```

### Rationale

Current-month burn may be misleading because hiring, GTM, or implementation costs may rise next month.

### CFO decision

If runway is below the minimum threshold, freeze non-critical spend and revisit fundraising timing.

---

## A5. Are we default alive or default dependent?

### Analysis needed

Ask whether the company can reach a self-sustaining or fundable milestone before cash runs out.

### Simple test

```text
Can we reach the next financing or revenue milestone before cash runs out, using realistic assumptions?
```

### Rationale

Pre-seed startups often assume they can raise later. The CFO review must test whether that assumption is realistic.

### CFO decision

If default dependent, define the raise timeline, bridge plan, or cost reduction plan.

---

## A6. What is our minimum cash buffer?

### Analysis needed

Set a minimum cash threshold that the company should not go below.

### Suggested policy

```text
Minimum Cash Buffer = 3 to 6 months of forward net burn
```

### Rationale

A company should not wait until it has one month of runway to make hard decisions.

### CFO decision

If cash is projected to fall below buffer, trigger a budget reduction or funding plan.

---

## A7. What is the 13-week cash forecast?

### Analysis needed

Build a week-by-week forecast for:

- payroll,
- vendor payments,
- contractor payments,
- cloud,
- tax/VAT,
- customer collections,
- expected invoices,
- one-time expenses.

### Rationale

Monthly models can hide short-term cash crunches.

### CFO decision

Prioritize collections and delay discretionary spend if a 13-week cash gap appears.

# 6. Section B — Burn Quality and Budget Variance

## B1. What did we plan to spend versus what did we actually spend?

### Analysis needed

Compare budget vs actual by function:

- Product/R&D,
- GTM,
- CS/Implementation,
- COGS,
- G&A/Compliance.

### Rationale

Variance tells whether the company is operating with financial control.

### CFO decision

Investigate any variance above an agreed threshold, such as 10% or SAR X.

---

## B2. Which function created the largest burn this month?

### Analysis needed

Rank spend by function.

### Rationale

The biggest burn category deserves the deepest operating review.

### CFO decision

If the largest burn category is not tied to the current milestone, reallocate or slow spend.

---

## B3. Which cost increased the fastest?

### Analysis needed

Compare month-over-month growth by cost line.

### Rationale

Fast-growing costs often become problems before leadership notices.

### CFO decision

Flag cost lines growing faster than revenue, pipeline, or customer activation.

---

## B4. Which costs were non-recurring versus recurring?

### Analysis needed

Separate:

- one-time costs,
- temporary project costs,
- monthly recurring costs,
- annual contracts,
- usage-based costs.

### Rationale

Recurring cost increases are more dangerous than one-time costs because they raise the future burn base.

### CFO decision

Avoid converting too many flexible costs into permanent fixed costs too early.

---

## B5. Which costs were discretionary?

### Analysis needed

Mark each cost as:

- mission-critical,
- important,
- optional,
- deferrable,
- cuttable.

### Rationale

If cash gets tight, the company must know what to cut without hurting core proof.

### CFO decision

Create a cut list before a crisis.

---

## B6. Did this month’s burn create evidence?

### Analysis needed

For each major cost, ask:

```text
What proof did this cost produce?
```

Examples:

| Cost | Expected proof |
|---|---|
| Engineering | Shipped MVP module |
| GTM | Qualified pipeline |
| CS | Activated customer |
| Marketing | Buyer education / meetings |
| Legal | Contract readiness |
| Cloud | Reliable customer environment |

### Rationale

The pre-seed company must turn cash into proof.

### CFO decision

Stop or reduce costs that repeatedly fail to produce evidence.

# 7. Section C — Revenue, ARR, Bookings, Cash, and Deferred Revenue

## C1. What ARR did we start and end the month with?

### Analysis needed

Track:

- beginning ARR,
- new ARR,
- expansion ARR,
- contraction ARR,
- churned ARR,
- ending ARR.

### Formula

```text
Ending ARR = Beginning ARR + New ARR + Expansion ARR - Contraction ARR - Churned ARR
```

### Rationale

ARR is the recurring revenue base investors will care about.

### CFO decision

If ARR is not growing, investigate whether the issue is pipeline, conversion, pricing, or activation.

---

## C2. What bookings did we sign this month?

### Analysis needed

Bookings include signed contractual commitments, separated by:

- subscription bookings,
- implementation bookings,
- pilot bookings,
- services bookings.

### Rationale

Bookings show commercial momentum, but they are not the same as cash or revenue.

### CFO decision

Compare bookings to collection timing and delivery obligations.

---

## C3. How much cash did we actually collect?

### Analysis needed

Track cash by source:

- annual upfront subscriptions,
- monthly subscriptions,
- implementation fees,
- pilot payments,
- services,
- VAT collected.

### Rationale

Cash collected funds runway. Bookings do not.

### CFO decision

If signed contracts are not collected quickly, fix payment terms or collection process.

---

## C4. What revenue should be recognized this month?

### Analysis needed

Separate:

- recognized subscription revenue,
- implementation revenue,
- services revenue,
- deferred revenue.

### Rationale

Annual upfront cash should not be treated as fully earned revenue in one month.

### CFO decision

Maintain clean investor reporting and avoid overstating revenue.

---

## C5. What is our deferred revenue balance?

### Analysis needed

Calculate cash collected for services not yet delivered.

### Formula

```text
Deferred Revenue = Cash Collected for Subscription - Revenue Recognized
```

### Rationale

Deferred revenue is a liability. It shows future service obligations.

### CFO decision

Ensure cash planning respects future delivery obligations.

---

## C6. What is our average ACV?

### Analysis needed

Calculate ACV by customer segment and package.

### Rationale

ACV determines whether the sales and implementation motion is economically viable.

### CFO decision

If ACV is too low for the sales/implementation effort, adjust pricing or ICP.

---

## C7. Are we discounting too much?

### Analysis needed

Track:

- list price,
- contracted price,
- discount percentage,
- reason for discount,
- approval owner.

### Rationale

Discounting may win early customers but can weaken positioning and future pricing.

### CFO decision

Create a discount policy and approval gate.

Compare response and conversion by:

- Head of Partnerships,
- CRO,
- CFO,
- RevOps,
- CEO/GM,
- partner managers.

### Rationale

The best persona should shape messaging, product demo, and sales motion.

### CFO decision

### Analysis needed

Track discovery-to-diagnostic conversion.

### Rationale

For this product, diagnostics are a critical sales stage because buyers need help understanding their operating pain.

### CFO decision

If demos happen without diagnostics, sales may become too feature-led and weak.

---

## D5. How many proposals were sent?

### Analysis needed

Track proposal count and value.

### Rationale

Proposals show commercial seriousness.

### CFO decision

If proposals do not convert, review pricing, value proof, and stakeholder alignment.

---

## D6. What is our win rate?

### Analysis needed

Calculate win rate from qualified opportunity to closed-won.

### Formula

```text
Win Rate = Closed-Won Opportunities / Qualified Opportunities
```

### Rationale

Win rate shows whether the GTM motion is working.

### CFO decision

If win rate is low, do not scale sales hiring yet.

---

## D7. What is our sales cycle?

### Analysis needed

Track days from first qualified meeting to signed contract.

### Rationale

Sales cycle affects cash planning and hiring needs.

### CFO decision

If sales cycle is long, increase runway planning and shorten pilot entry.

---

## D8. What is our pipeline coverage?

### Analysis needed

Compare qualified pipeline to next-quarter ARR target.

### Formula

```text
Pipeline Coverage = Qualified Pipeline / ARR Target
```

### Rationale

If win rate is 25%, the company may need around 4x pipeline coverage.

### CFO decision

If coverage is weak, focus on pipeline before hiring closing capacity.

---

## D9. What is CAC this month and quarter?

### Analysis needed

Include:

- sales salaries,
- founder GTM allocation,
- marketing spend,
- tools,
- events,
- travel,
- commissions,
- solution consulting allocation.

### Formula

```text
CAC = Sales and Marketing Spend / New Customers
```

### Rationale

CAC tells how expensive customer acquisition is.

### CFO decision

If CAC is rising without better ACV or conversion, freeze GTM expansion.

---

## D10. What is CAC payback?

### Analysis needed

Use gross profit, not revenue alone.

### Formula

```text
CAC Payback = CAC / Monthly Gross Profit per Customer
```

### Rationale

CAC payback shows how long it takes to recover acquisition cost.

### CFO decision

If payback is too long for pre-seed runway, improve pricing, reduce CAC, or target higher ACV accounts.

- kickoff scheduled,
- data received,
- workspace configured,
- users trained,
- first workflow launched,
- activated.

### Rationale

Implementation is where signed revenue becomes real value.

### Analysis needed

Measure days from contract signed to kickoff.

### Rationale

Long delay after signing creates customer excitement decay.

### CFO decision

Fix sales-to-CS handoff if kickoff is delayed.

- first claim submitted,

- first accepted claim,
- first protection window,
- first payout eligibility preview,
- first dashboard review.

### Rationale

Time to first value is one of the strongest predictors of activation and retention.

### CFO decision

If time to value is long, simplify onboarding or reduce first workflow scope.

## F4. What is the customer activation score?

- partner registry uploaded,
- users trained,
- first claim submitted,
- claim reviewed,
- sales confirmation used,
- protection window applied,
- payout preview generated,
- dashboard reviewed,
- weekly cadence started.

## F5. What implementation hours were used?

- data import,
- agreement setup,
- workflow configuration,
- training,
- dashboard setup,

### CFO decision

## F6. What is implementation margin?

```text
Implementation Margin =
(Implementation Fee - Implementation Delivery Cost) / Implementation Fee
```

### Rationale

Implementation should not become free consulting.

### CFO decision

If implementation margin is negative, pricing or scope must change.

- signed contract,
- invoiced,
- cash collected.

### Rationale

Only signed and collectible contracts support the financial model.

### CFO decision

Do not count verbal commitments as bookings.

---

## J2. What customer obligations have we accepted?

### Analysis needed

Track:

- promised features,
- implementation timeline,
- custom work,
- security obligations,
- SLA commitments,
- data obligations,
- termination rights.

### Rationale

Commercial promises create future cost and risk.

### CFO decision

Review whether customer obligations match pricing.

---

## J3. Are we taking too much custom risk?

### Analysis needed

Identify custom terms, custom integrations, custom workflows, or custom reporting.

### Rationale

Custom work can destroy the product roadmap and gross margin.

### CFO decision

Charge for custom work or reject it.

# 15. Section K — Pre-Seed Investor Readiness

## K1. What proof would we show an investor this month?

### Analysis needed

Build monthly investor proof:

- product shipped,
- customer interviews,
- paid pilots,
- ARR,
- cash collected,
- activation metrics,
- pipeline,
- references,
- burn discipline,
- roadmap progress.

### Rationale

Pre-seed investors fund proof trajectory, not just idea quality.

### CFO decision

If investor proof is weak, focus next month on proof creation rather than expansion.

---

## K2. Are we building a fundable story?

### Analysis needed

The fundable story should show:

```text
Clear problem
+ sharp ICP
+ working MVP
+ paying customers
+ customer activation
+ repeatable GTM learning
+ disciplined burn
+ strong next milestone
```

### Rationale

A funding round needs a coherent narrative backed by evidence.

### CFO decision

Identify the weakest part of the story and assign an owner.

---

## K3. What milestone must we hit before raising?

### Analysis needed

Choose milestone type:

| Milestone | Example |
|---|---|
| Product milestone | MVP live with full claim workflow |
| Commercial milestone | SAR X ARR or X paid customers |
| Activation milestone | X customers activated |
| Reference milestone | X referenceable customers |
| Efficiency milestone | burn below SAR X with pipeline/ARR growth |
| Enterprise readiness milestone | security/procurement documents ready |

### Rationale

Raising without a clear milestone weakens investor confidence.

### CFO decision

Set the next raise milestone and monthly path to reach it.

---

## K4. What is our current funding need?

### Analysis needed

Calculate:

- cash required to reach milestone,
- cash buffer,
- downside case,
- hiring plan,
- working capital,
- legal/compliance cost.

### Formula

```text
Funding Need = Maximum Cash Deficit + Target Cash Buffer
```

### Rationale

The raise amount should be milestone-based, not guessed.

### CFO decision

Define raise amount, timing, and use of funds.

# 20. CFO Decision Gates

## Gate 1 — Hiring

Approve hiring only if:

- role removes a proven bottleneck,
- cash supports the hire,
- milestone depends on the hire,
- contractor alternative was considered,
- runway remains above threshold after hire.

## Gate 2 — GTM Spend

Increase GTM spend only if:

- ICP is clear,
- qualified pipeline is growing,
- win rate is improving,
- activation is strong enough,
- CAC is monitored.

## Gate 3 — Product Expansion

Approve new product modules only if:

- customer need is repeatable,
- module supports core wedge,
- module improves paid conversion, retention, or ACV,
- engineering capacity exists,
- module does not delay MVP proof.

## Gate 4 — Integrations

Build integrations only if:

- paid customers require them,
- scope is repeatable,
- ACV justifies build,
- integration does not become custom services.

## Gate 5 — Fundraising

Start fundraising when:

- story is clear,
- proof score is improving,
- runway allows enough process time,
- financial model is updated,
- investor materials reflect current traction.

# 21. Final CFO Principle

The monthly CFO review should not feel like accounting.

It should feel like a control room.

The question is not only:

```text
How much did we spend?
```

The real question is:

```text
Did our spend move the company closer to pre-seed proof, fundability, and a repeatable business?
```

For Partner Revenue OS, the best monthly CFO review connects:

```text
Cash
→ Burn
→ Product progress
→ GTM progress
→ Customer activation
→ Unit economics
→ Saudi operating readiness
→ Investor readiness
```

That is how the company protects runway while building proof.
